# Strategy 001

## Thesis

**S001** is a trading strategy grounded in **actual, concrete, market-agnostic** mathematical models. Rather than being designed around a specific signal or indicator, S001 works in reverse: it uses **integration over uncertainty ranges** to determine what win rate and R:R ratio a strategy *must* achieve in order to maximise **expected log-wealth growth** — the correct objective for compounding capital — given a fixed capital base, fee structure, and leverage, while also penalising for **strategy implementation difficulty**.

> **Clarification:** strategy implementation difficulty is denoted $D_i$. It is a functional measure of how sensitive the strategy's expected growth is to mis-estimation of its parameters — not a measure of EV itself. A strategy with tight tolerances on $w$ and $r$ (i.e. one that collapses badly if your estimates are even slightly wrong) has high $D_i$ and should be penalised accordingly. Formal definition in §2.1.

> **Note on optimisation target:** EV maximisation alone is the wrong objective for a repeated-bet compounding context — it ignores variance and leads to overbetting and eventual ruin. The correct objective is maximising the **Expected Growth Rate** $G$, defined as the expected log-wealth increment per trade. Kelly sizing falls out naturally from this. $EV$ is preserved as a diagnostic tool, not as the primary optimisation target.

> **Note on mathematical method:** double integration over $(w, r)$ uncertainty ranges is the primary tool for computing $\mathbb{E}[G]$ and $D_i$.

## Phase I — Constraints & Ground Rules

S001 will be deployed on a VPS with starting capital $X = 500$€. The exchange is **Bitget**, trading **perpetual futures contracts**. The following rules are strategy-performance-agnostic — they hold regardless of what signal logic we eventually design:

**Rule 1 — Low trade frequency.**
Bitget perpetual futures carry non-trivial fees. Overtrading erodes edge faster than any signal weakness. Low frequency is a structural requirement, not a stylistic preference.

**Rule 2 — Limit orders exclusively.**
Limit orders on Bitget are approximately 60% cheaper than market orders. Given that fees are baked into the mathematical model, using market orders would invalidate our fee assumptions. All entries and exits must use limit orders. The round-trip fee rate is therefore fixed at $f_r = 0.0004$.

**Rule 3 — Large-move dependency.**
Because frequency is low, each trade must carry substantial weight. This biases the strategy toward: a) trend following, b) breakout trading, c) mean reversion, or d) a principled blend. The choice among these will be made in Phase III, after Phase II establishes the required $(w, r)$ operating window.

> The win rate $w$ is not a single point — it is an interval $[w_{\text{low}}, w_{\text{high}}]$ reflecting genuine uncertainty about forward performance. Any strategy that only works at a single precise $w$ value is too fragile to deploy. Robustness across the full interval is a hard requirement.

## Phase II — Mathematical Model

The objective of Phase II is to produce a rigorous, closed mathematical specification that any trading algorithm implemented under S001 must satisfy. The outputs are: a formal $D_i$ definition, the growth rate function $G(w, r, f)$, its expectation over uncertainty ranges via double integration, the optimal Kelly fraction $f^*$, and a summary constraints table.

### 2.1 — Quantifying $D_i$

$D_i$ must capture something that $EV$ and $G$ do not — namely, how much the strategy's performance degrades when our parameter estimates are wrong. A strategy that is robust to estimation error has low $D_i$; a fragile one has high $D_i$.

The previous candidate $D_i = w \cdot r$ is discarded: it is proportional to EV and adds no independent information.

Instead, we define $D_i$ as a **sensitivity functional** — a first-order measure of how much $G$ varies in response to perturbations in $w$ and $r$:

$$\boxed{D_i(w, r, f) = \left|\frac{\partial G}{\partial w}\right| \cdot \Delta w + \left|\frac{\partial G}{\partial r}\right| \cdot \Delta r}$$

Where $\Delta w$ and $\Delta r$ are the half-widths of our uncertainty intervals — i.e. $\Delta w = \frac{w_{\text{high}} - w_{\text{low}}}{2}$ and analogously for $r$. The partial derivatives are evaluated at the interval midpoints $\bar{w}$, $\bar{r}$.

Intuitively: $D_i$ is the expected first-order drop in $G$ if our parameter estimates are off by their typical uncertainty. A low-$D_i$ strategy stays profitable even when you're wrong about $w$ and $r$. That is a real and distinct constraint from simply having high $G$.

The partial derivatives will be computed explicitly in §2.4 once $G$ is fully defined.

### 2.2 — Why $G$, not $EV$

$EV$ measures the average dollar return per trade. But dollar return is the wrong thing to maximise when you are reinvesting and compounding. Consider: a strategy with $EV = +50$€ but 80% standard deviation will destroy a 500€ account in a drawdown long before the expectation materialises. The St. Petersburg paradox formalises exactly this failure.

The correct objective for a sequence of reinvested trades is the **expected logarithmic wealth increment per trade**, or Expected Growth Rate:

$$G = \mathbb{E}[\log(1 + f \cdot \tilde{X})]$$

where $f$ is the fraction of capital risked and $\tilde{X}$ is the net return random variable per unit risked. For our binary win/loss model this becomes:

$$\boxed{G(w, r, f) = w \cdot \log(1 + f \cdot r_{\text{net}}) + (1 - w) \cdot \log(1 - f \cdot r_{\text{risk}})}$$

Where the fee-adjusted returns are:

$$r_{\text{net}} = r - L \cdot f_r \qquad \text{(net reward per unit risked, after fees)}$$
$$r_{\text{risk}} = r_2 + L \cdot f_r \qquad \text{(net risk per unit risked, after fees)}$$

And the parameters carry over from the EV model: $r$ is the reward ratio, $r_2$ is the risk ratio (typically $r_2 = 1$), $L$ is leverage, $f_r = 0.0004$ is the round-trip limit order fee rate on Bitget.

Note that $G > 0$ is the fundamental viability condition — a strategy with $G \leq 0$ destroys capital in expectation on a log scale, regardless of its raw $EV$.

### 2.3 — EV as a Diagnostic (Preserved from Original)

Although $EV$ is no longer the primary optimisation target, it remains a useful sanity check and diagnostic. For completeness, the full fee-aware $EV$ model derived originally is preserved here:

$$\boxed{EV(X) = \left[w \cdot (r \cdot X)\right] - \left[(1-w) \cdot (r_2 \cdot X)\right] - \left[X \cdot L \cdot (f_{\text{entry}} + f_{\text{exit}})\right]}$$

With volatility-anchored reward and risk:

$$r = n \cdot \sigma, \qquad r = m \cdot r_2$$

where $n$ is the noise multiplier ($n = 2$ or $3$ are standard) and $m$ is the desired reward multiple over risk. This anchors stop and target placement to actual market volatility rather than arbitrary pip distances — a sound approach retained in S001.

The Kelly Criterion gives the optimal fraction of capital to risk per trade as a function of $w$, $r$, $r_2$:

$$\boxed{K^* = \frac{w \cdot r - (1 - w) \cdot r_2}{r}}$$

Note that $K^*$ is derived by maximising $G$ with respect to $f$, setting $\frac{\partial G}{\partial f} = 0$. In the binary model above this yields exactly the formula above, which confirms the internal consistency between §2.2 and §2.3.

> **Practical note:** raw Kelly is aggressive and known to produce large drawdowns. In practice, **fractional Kelly** — typically $\frac{1}{2} K^*$ or $\frac{1}{4} K^*$ — is used to reduce variance at the cost of slightly slower growth. S001 will use $f = \frac{1}{2} K^*$ as the default position sizing rule.

### 2.4 — Double Integration over $(w, r)$ Uncertainty

In practice, we never know $w$ and $r$ exactly. We have estimates from backtests and forward tests, which give us intervals. Rather than optimising at a point, we optimise $\mathbb{E}[G]$ over those intervals, treating $w$ and $r$ as uniformly distributed on $[w_{\text{low}}, w_{\text{high}}]$ and $[r_{\text{low}}, r_{\text{high}}]$ respectively.

The expected growth rate under parameter uncertainty is:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[G](f) = \frac{1}{(w_{\text{high}} - w_{\text{low}})(r_{\text{high}} - r_{\text{low}})} \int_{w_{\text{low}}}^{w_{\text{high}}} \int_{r_{\text{low}}}^{r_{\text{high}}} G(w, r, f) \, dr \, dw}$$

Substituting $G$:

$$\mathbb{E}[G](f) = \frac{1}{\Delta W \cdot \Delta R} \int_{w_{\text{low}}}^{w_{\text{high}}} \int_{r_{\text{low}}}^{r_{\text{high}}} \left[ w \cdot \log(1 + f \cdot r_{\text{net}}) + (1-w) \cdot \log(1 - f \cdot r_{\text{risk}}) \right] dr \, dw$$

Where $\Delta W = w_{\text{high}} - w_{\text{low}}$ and $\Delta R = r_{\text{high}} - r_{\text{low}}$.

Since $r_{\text{net}} = r - L \cdot f_r$ depends on $r$ but $w$ appears linearly in $G$, the double integral separates cleanly. Define:

$$\bar{w} = \frac{w_{\text{low}} + w_{\text{high}}}{2} \qquad \text{(midpoint win rate)}$$

Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[G](f) = \frac{1}{\Delta R} \int_{r_{\text{low}}}^{r_{\text{high}}} \left[ \bar{w} \cdot \log(1 + f(r - Lf_r)) + (1 - \bar{w}) \cdot \log(1 - f(r_2 + Lf_r)) \right] dr$$

The second log term is constant in $r$ (since $r_2$ is fixed), so the integral reduces to:

$$\mathbb{E}[G](f) = \frac{\bar{w}}{\Delta R} \int_{r_{\text{low}}}^{r_{\text{high}}} \log(1 + f(r - Lf_r)) \, dr + (1 - \bar{w}) \cdot \log(1 - f \cdot r_{\text{risk}})$$

The remaining integral has the closed form $\int \log(1 + ar) \, dr = \frac{(1+ar)\log(1+ar) - (1+ar)}{a} + C$, giving us a fully closed expression for $\mathbb{E}[G](f)$ as a function of $f$ and our interval bounds.

**Optimal sizing $f^*$** is then found numerically by solving $\frac{d}{df}\mathbb{E}[G](f) = 0$, or simply by evaluating $\mathbb{E}[G](f)$ on a fine grid over $f \in (0, 1)$ and taking the argmax.

**$D_i$ with partial derivatives explicitly computed:**

From $G(w, r, f)$:

$$\frac{\partial G}{\partial w} = \log(1 + f \cdot r_{\text{net}}) - \log(1 - f \cdot r_{\text{risk}})$$

$$\frac{\partial G}{\partial r} = \frac{w \cdot f}{1 + f \cdot r_{\text{net}}}$$

Both evaluated at $(\bar{w}, \bar{r}, f^*)$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{D_i = \left[\log(1 + f^* r_{\text{net}}) - \log(1 - f^* r_{\text{risk}})\right] \cdot \Delta w + \frac{\bar{w} \cdot f^*}{1 + f^* r_{\text{net}}} \cdot \Delta r}$$

A strategy is considered **robust** if $D_i < \epsilon \cdot G(\bar{w}, \bar{r}, f^*)$ for some tolerance $\epsilon$ — i.e. the first-order degradation is small relative to the nominal growth rate. A reasonable starting value is $\epsilon = 0.25$: the strategy must maintain at least 75% of its nominal $G$ under typical estimation error.

### 2.5 — Results & Constraints Table

Using the model above, we can numerically evaluate $\mathbb{E}[G]$, $f^*$, and $D_i$ for any strategy candidate by plugging in its $(w_{\text{low}}, w_{\text{high}}, r_{\text{low}}, r_{\text{high}})$ bounds. Before Phase III signal design, we fix the **minimum acceptable constraints** that any S001-compliant algorithm must satisfy:

| Parameter | Constraint | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| $G(\bar{w}, \bar{r}, f^*)$ | $> 0$ | Non-negotiable viability condition |
| $\mathbb{E}[G](f^*)$ | $> 0$ over full uncertainty range | Robust profitability under uncertainty |
| $w_{\text{low}}$ | $\geq 0.40$ | Minimum floor win rate for $G > 0$ at $r \geq 1$ |
| $r_{\text{low}}$ | $\geq 1.5$ | Minimum reward:risk; below this $G < 0$ unless $w$ is unrealistically high |
| $f^* \leq \frac{1}{2} K^*$ | Enforced | Fractional Kelly to limit drawdown |
| $D_i$ | $< 0.25 \cdot G(\bar{w}, \bar{r}, f^*)$ | Robustness tolerance |
| Trade frequency | Low (target: $\leq 5$/day) | Fee minimisation per Rule 1 |
| Order type | Limit only | Fee minimisation per Rule 2 |
| $f_r$ | $0.0004$ | Bitget limit order round-trip fee |

### 2.6 — Conclusion

Phase II establishes that S001's optimisation target is the **Expected Growth Rate** $G$, not raw $EV$. The double integral over $(w, r)$ uncertainty intervals gives a fee-aware, leverage-aware, volatility-anchored expected growth function whose maximum defines both the optimal Kelly fraction $f^*$ and — through its partial derivatives — the robustness measure $D_i$.

Any strategy entering Phase III must be able to credibly claim a $(w_{\text{low}}, w_{\text{high}}, r_{\text{low}}, r_{\text{high}})$ operating window that satisfies all constraints in §2.5. Signal logic, indicator choice, and entry conditions are all subordinate to these requirements — they are the means of achieving the required window, not ends in themselves.

Phase III will select a signal approach from among trend following, breakout, mean reversion, or a blend — evaluated strictly on its ability to produce a compliant $(w, r)$ window in backtesting.
